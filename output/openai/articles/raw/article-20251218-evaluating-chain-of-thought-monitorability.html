Evaluating chain-of-thought monitorability

We introduce evaluations for chain-of-thought monitorability and study how it scales with test-time compute, reinforcement learning, and pretraining.

Read the paper(opens in a new window)
Loading

When AI systems make decisions that are difficult to supervise directly, it becomes important to understand how those decisions are made. One promising approach is to monitor a models internal reasoning, rather than only its actions or final outputs.

Modern reasoning models, such as GPT5 Thinking, generate an explicit chain-of-thought before producing an answer. Monitoring these chains-of-thought for misbehavior can be far more effective than monitoring a models actions and outputs alone. However, researchers at OpenAI and across the broader industry worry(opens in a new window) that this chain-of-thought monitorability may be fragile to changes in training procedure, data sources, and even continued scaling of existing algorithms.

We want chain-of-thought monitorability to hold up as models scale and are deployed in higher-stakes settings. We call on researchers across the industry to work to preserve chain-of-thought monitorability as long as possible and to determine whether it can serve as a load-bearing control layer for future AI systems. To preserve monitorability, we must be able to measure it, yet few evaluations exist [1(opens in a new window), 2(opens in a new window), 3(opens in a new window)] and methodologies vary.

In this work, we study chain-of-thought monitorability systematically. We introduce a framework to evaluate chain-of-thought monitorability and a suite of 13 evaluations (comprising a total of 24 environments) designed to concretely measure the monitorability of a system. These evaluations fall into three archetypesintervention, process, and outcome-property. Using them, we study how monitorable todays large language models are, and how monitorability changes with more reasoning at inference time, larger reinforcement learning runs, and increased pretraining scale.

We find that most frontier reasoning models are fairly monitorable, though not perfectly so. In almost every setting we study, monitoring chains-of-thought is substantially more effective than monitoring actions and final outputs alone. Models that think for longer tend to be more monitorable, and reinforcement learning optimization at current frontier scales does not appear to meaningfully degrade monitorability.

Notably, we observe a tradeoff between reasoning effort and model size: when a model is run at low reasoning effort, a smaller model run at higher reasoning effort can reach comparable capability while being easier to monitor. This comes at the cost of increased inference compute, which we refer to as a monitorability tax. Finally, we find that asking follow-up questions and monitoring the resulting chains- of- thought can further improve monitorability.

What is Monitorability?

We define monitorability as the ability of a monitor to predict properties of interest about an agents behavior. In many cases, these properties correspond to forms of misbehaviorsuch as deception, reward hacking, or biased reasoningbut they can also include benign properties, such as whether an agent relied on a particular hint or followed a specific line of reasoning.

Monitorability is a two-sided property. It depends both on the monitor but also on the agent being monitored.

  • Monitors. We could make monitors smarter in various ways, and we would like our evaluations to give higher scores to monitors that can make better predictions about an agents behavior.
  • Agents. There are many observations of the agent that could be given to the monitor, including the agents actions, outputs, neural activations, and recently with the advent of reasoning models, the chain-of-thought. Its possible for some agents to have thoughts that are harder to understand than others, and so we would like our monitorability evaluations to be able to compare agents and not just monitors.

Our evaluations are designed to assess both sides: whether certain agents are easier or harder to monitor, and whether more capable monitors can extract more information from the same signals.

Why should chains-of-thought be monitorable?

Before the current generation of reasoning models, chains-of-thought were typically elicited by prompting a model to think step by step.

The chains-of-thought of reasoning models differ in two important ways. First, they are explicitly trained to generate a chain-of-thought and then give an answer. When prompting a chat model to think step by step, the model has to adhere to any specifications placed on the model, i.e. to be helpful, harmless, etc. Conversely, the reinforcement learning training process teaches a reasoning model that the CoT is a private space where it can think whatever it wants and not be punished, just like a human can think privately and not be punished for thought crimes.

Second, the training process effectively searches for chains-of-thought that are the most instrumentally useful to the agent in getting a high reward final answer. Thus, not only is the chain-of-thought a space where the model can freely express itself, it contains thinking that helps the model reach its final answer.

A framework for evaluating monitorability

We organize our monitorability evaluations into three distinct archetypes, each exploiting a different mechanism to measure monitorability.

  1. Intervention evaluations, inspired by Chen et al. 2025(opens in a new window), Chua et al. 2025(opens in a new window), Turpin et al. 2023(opens in a new window), we introduce a controlled change in the agents environment and test whether a monitor can detect why behavior changed by identifying evidence of the intervention in the chain-of-thought. Unlike standard control evaluations (e.g. Shade-Arena(opens in a new window)), intervention evaluations allow us to probe whether a monitor can detect why behavior changed, rather than simply detecting whether a behavior occurred.
  2. Process evaluations focus on tasks with a limited number of valid solution paths (for example, a math problem can only be solved with certain theorems). Human experts enumerate these paths, and we test whether a monitor can detect which steps the model actually took. These evaluations allow us to link what a model did to particular steps the model must have taken and ask if those steps are monitorable.
  3. Outcome-property evaluations apply when a property of the models output can be reliably measured. In these cases, we test whether a monitor can predict that propertysuch as reward hackingfrom the models reasoning or actions. For example, in a coding task, we may have a way to reliably evaluate whether a model is taking actions to hack a reward signal, and we can ask if a monitor finds evidence of intent to reward hack in the models thinking or actions.

Below we provide a taxonomy for our evaluations. Please see our paper for a full description of each individual evaluation.

Neutral evaluations ask whether the reasoning for normal tasks where the model does not misbehave is monitorable. Sycophancy evaluations target cases where models change their behavior to overly please or agree with a user. Bias evaluations ask if a models bias around gender, race, etc. is monitorable. Finally, we use the misalignment label as a catchall for other forms of misbehavior: cheating, lying, etc.

The effect of high compute reinforcement learning

Treating the chain-of-thought as a private workspaceand training models to use it to solve tasks effectivelyshould, in principle, make chains-of-thought monitorable.At the same time, there are several reasons why reinforcement learning could plausibly harm chain-of-thought monitorability.

First, constraints on reasoning length, such as limiting the time or tokens a model has to think, create incentives for models to reason less. Second, even without applying direct optimization pressure to the chain-of-thought itself, indirect pressures could still reduce monitorability. For example(opens in a new window), models may find it easier to reach high-reward answers if they avoid reasoning paths that can result in low-reward or undesirable outcomes. Finally, reduced monitorability could arise from spurious drift driven by reinforcement learning gradient noise.

Below, we examine how monitorability changes over two of OpenAIs frontier reinforcement learning training runs. While these results represent only two datapoints, they suggest reinforcement learningoptimization at current frontier scales does not materially degrade chain-of-thought monitorability, and increases monitorability for early steps. However, this does not rule out the possibility that significantly larger reinforcement learning scales could negatively affect monitorability in the future.

Effect of pretraining scale

A large portion of the intelligence of current AI systems is driven by increased pretraining scale, whereby both model parameters and dataset sizes have consistently grown since the beginning of the GPT paradigm. Increased parameter counts results in more inference compute being used per token generated, which, in the case of reasoning models, gives more opportunity for the models cognition to take place within activations rather than within the chain-of-thought. Thus, we might expect larger models to be less monitorable at a fixed capability level.

At the same time, smaller models may also be less capable of using the chain-of-thought effectivelywhich may also affect monitorability.

To investigate the relationship between pretraining size, capability, and monitorability, we trained a family of models over two orders of magnitude of pretraining compute with increasing depth and width, and then applied reinforcement learning to each on a shared dataset of math, science, and coding tasks. We evaluated each model across a range of reasoning efforts and compared monitorability at matched capability levels.

We plot the monitorability on our evaluation suite of each model versus the mean pass rate over a set of standard capability evaluations (GPQA-Diamond, AIME 2025, and CodeForces). Each model is evaluated over many reasoning efforts (note that each point in this figure is actually the result of a linear interpolation between measured reasoning efforts). Circles indicate that the model at that particular reasoning effort dominates in monitorability at that capability level, and transparent Xs represent that the model at that particular reasoning effort is dominated in monitorability by another model for that capability level. In black arrows we show examples of two ways to spend more inference compute, by sampling many more tokens with a smaller model or relatively less additional tokens with the same model. In red arrows, we show the monitorability tax that must be paid between, i.e. slightly reduced capabilities for far more monitorability.

In general, we find that if we were to deploy a given model at a low reasoning effort, we could instead deploy a smaller model at a higher reasoning effort. Compared to spending the additional compute on more reasoning effort from the original large model, we could obtain a higher monitorability at a small capability cost, or monitorability tax; however, the wallclock time it takes to finish a completion may also be a factor in some deployment decisions, which we do not consider here. As our models improve and are deployed in higher stakes settings, it may become necessary to pay such a tax to safely deploy them.

These results have the following limitations: (1) we only train each model once, and therefore do not account for variance across training runs. For example, the medium model seems fairly off-trend in monitorability compared to the other models in the series, which could be due to this. (2) We evaluate capabilities on relatively in-distribution settings (math, science, and coding), and the tradeoffs may change when accounting for out-of-distribution robustness.

Agent versus monitor compute and scalable control

As AI systems scale beyond human-level performance, direct human supervision becomes increasingly difficult. Ideally, we would have scalable control procedures that allow trusted but weaker monitors to oversee stronger, potentially untrusted agents. Such procedures should also be able to absorb additional test-time compute and produce more accurate predictions.

Getting more monitorability out of chains-of-thought

Chain-of-thought monitorability is often treated as a fixed quantity: once a model produces a response at a given reasoning effort, one monitors the resulting chain-of-thought as best as possible. Because weve consistently found that monitorability improves as we increase the reasoning effort of our agents, we were inspired to ask if we could somehow get more tokens to monitor.

To explore this, we experimented with asking follow-up questions after a model responds, while keeping the original prompt, chain-of-thought, and answer in context. These follow-ups focus on the property being monitoredfor example, Did you use the hint in a meaningful way to choose your answer? or Did you do something sketchy? In principle, because the model retains access to its earlier reasoning, it may be able to surface previously unverbalized thoughts in the follow-up chain-of-thought or response.

These results are preliminary. We explored only a small number of follow-up question formats and believe there is substantial room for improved methodology. Furthermore, this method is similar in spirit to model confessions, and we are actively working on comparing the approaches.

Beyond improving monitorability, this approach is appealing because it can be applied post hoc, incurring additional compute cost only when deeper inspection is needed. Looking ahead, if models are trained to expect changes in reasoning effort mid-conversation, post-hoc follow-ups at higher reasoning effort may further improve monitorability even when initial reasoning effort is low or absent.

Limitations

Each evaluation in our suite measures whether a single aspect of the reasoning process is monitorable. While we aim to cover a broad range of behaviorsincluding sycophancy, sandbagging, bias, scheming, and reward hackingthere are many additional properties that remain untested.

As with any benchmark, our evaluations may suffer from limited realism or increasing evaluation awareness, and results may not generalize to detecting misbehavior in the wild.

Finally, as alignment improves, models may misbehave less frequently in controlled evaluation settings,reducing the signal available to monitorability and control evaluations that rely on detecting misbehavior. In those cases, neutral monitorability evaluations remain applicable, though it is an open question how predictive they are of real-world failures.

Conclusion

If the alignment problem cannot be fully solved, then scalable control methods will be incredibly important in our ability to safely deploy highly capable models. Chain-of-thought monitoring and related methods may be one of the few control mechanisms at our disposal. Chain-of-thought can also yield large benefits in understanding more about our models, e.g. whether they are aware they are in an evaluation and behaving differently than they would in deployment, whether there is evidence of any adverse goals that have not yet manifested in our evaluations, etc. Thus, webelieve it is of utmost importance to understand how and when we may lose monitorability.

We view chain-of-thought monitoring as complementary to mechanistic interpretability, not as a replacement for it. Like all known control and interpretability methods, each has limitations and blind spots. Our expectation is that combining multiple approachesa defense-in-depth strategycan help cover gaps that any single method leaves exposed. Notably, the evaluations introduced in this work could also be applied to assess and compare mechanistic interpretability-based monitoring techniques.

Because we believe that chain-of-thought monitoring could be a load-bearing layer in a scalable control scheme, we plan to expand upon these evaluations and use them to inform future modeling and data decisions.
